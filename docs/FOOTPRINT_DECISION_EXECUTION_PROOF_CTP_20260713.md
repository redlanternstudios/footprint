## THREE PASS ANALYSIS

### PASS 1: VISIBLE SOLUTION

Create a standard build form. Ask agents to fill in the problem, requirements, stories, tests, proof, lesson, and post idea. Store everything. Generate content after each build.

### PASS 2: CHALLENGE

That approach can become paperwork that agents complete after decisions are already made. It can also produce confident stories from weak evidence, reveal a proprietary pattern through several individually safe posts, or flood the audience every time a small task closes.

The public content formula can also become repetitive. If every post says that a common belief is wrong, the brand becomes combative. If every result becomes content, the audience becomes tired. If raw reactions control learning, Footprint will gradually favor famous contributors, broad controversy, and studio promotion.

### PASS 3: GOVERNING TRUTH

The system must capture only decision grade truth at the moment it matters. Each stage must have a required input, an accountable role, a binary gate, and a receipt. Proof must exist before a public lesson can exist.

The public method must challenge weak systems or incomplete beliefs, never attack people. A useful challenge earns attention because it gives the reader a better way to see a real problem. Proof earns trust. A practical rule earns saves and shares. A specific experience question invites substantive comments without begging for engagement.

## GOVERNING RECOMMENDATION

Build one canonical Build Record that travels through the complete lifecycle. Do not create separate project management truth, proof truth, and content truth.

The Build Record is the private source. The Public Proof Projection is a restricted view derived from it. Footprint may reason from the projection but may never receive fields classified as private.

## TEN LAYER ANALYSIS

### DECISION A: ONE CANONICAL BUILD RECORD

1. Surface: Store build identity, problem, root cause, use cases, solution intent, requirements, risks, stories, tests, proof, handoff, observation, and public lesson in one linked record.
2. Root cause: Important context currently lives across chats, code, receipts, memory, and human recollection.
3. First order effect: Every agent reads the same current decision state.
4. Second order effect: Review and quality can verify the reason, implementation, and proof together.
5. Third order effect: Footprint receives a reliable stream of lessons without asking builders to recreate the story later.
6. Upstream dependency: Product lane, authority, product identifier, owner, evidence source, and privacy classification.
7. Downstream dependency: Workflow state, agent tasks, receipts, search, analytics, content generation, correction, and deletion.
8. Failure modes: One giant record becomes noisy, agents overwrite history, or the record becomes a second truth source that conflicts with Claudex.
9. Recovery: Append decisions and evidence, version material changes, store references to canonical Claudex and repository artifacts, and never copy bridge state into the product repository.
10. Strategic implication: The Build Record becomes connective tissue, not a replacement for code, bridge state, or receipts.

### DECISION B: ROOT CAUSE GATE BEFORE BUILD

1. Surface: No build enters execution until the affected person, current behavior, undesired outcome, impact, frequency, root cause, and constraints are recorded.
2. Root cause: Teams often automate the visible task without understanding the actual failure.
3. First order effect: Builders solve a named problem.
4. Second order effect: Non goals become clearer and scope expansion becomes easier to detect.
5. Third order effect: Public lessons become specific because they begin with a real tension rather than generic advice.
6. Upstream dependency: Intake evidence and access to the people or system affected.
7. Downstream dependency: Use cases, requirements, story selection, acceptance criteria, and proof design.
8. Failure modes: Agents invent a root cause, or use a vague phrase such as inefficient process.
9. Recovery: TRUTH labels the root cause PARTIAL or UNKNOWN and returns the record to intake with the evidence needed.
10. Strategic implication: Blocking a weak start saves more time than accelerating the wrong build.

### DECISION C: PROOF CAPTURE DURING EXECUTION

1. Surface: Define the expected proof before implementation and capture it during testing and observation.
2. Root cause: After a build ships, evidence is difficult to reconstruct and claims become inflated.
3. First order effect: Acceptance criteria become observable and binary.
4. Second order effect: Results can be separated from anecdotes and hopes.
5. Third order effect: Footprint can explain what was learned even when the expected result did not occur.
6. Upstream dependency: Baseline, success signal, test environment, and allowed evidence.
7. Downstream dependency: Release verdict, outcome measurement, case study, post candidate, and future prioritization.
8. Failure modes: Screenshots without context, self reported results treated as measured results, or a demo treated as production proof.
9. Recovery: Store evidence type, source, time, environment, confidence, and claim ceiling. Unsupported claims remain disabled.
10. Strategic implication: Failed experiments can still create useful learning, but they cannot be presented as successful outcomes.

### DECISION D: PUBLIC PROOF PROJECTION

1. Surface: Create a separate public safe projection after truth, rights, privacy, and intellectual property gates pass.
2. Root cause: The complete build record contains information that a content agent should never see.
3. First order effect: Content creation begins with already limited material.
4. Second order effect: A model error cannot reveal a field that was never included in its input.
5. Third order effect: Correction and removal can trace every public sentence to its permitted source.
6. Upstream dependency: Field classification, asset rights, contributor consent, identity authority, evidence status, and historical exposure ledger.
7. Downstream dependency: Post generation, editorial review, publishing, owned pages, and audit receipts.
8. Failure modes: A field is classified too broadly, two safe details combine into a private pattern, or a later policy change leaves an old projection active.
9. Recovery: Default deny, cumulative leakage scan, policy version binding, expiry, retraction, and regeneration.
10. Strategic implication: Intellectual property protection must operate across the publication history, not only one post at a time.

### DECISION E: USEFUL CONTRARIAN PROOF

1. Surface: Use a five part public structure: familiar belief, useful challenge, verified proof, practical rule, and specific question.
2. Root cause: Generic updates rarely give readers a reason to pause, respond, remember, or share.
3. First order effect: The opening creates useful tension.
4. Second order effect: Proof prevents the tension from becoming empty opinion.
5. Third order effect: A reusable rule lets readers help themselves or someone else.
6. Upstream dependency: Safe proof, audience relevance, voice authority, and a lesson that changes a decision.
7. Downstream dependency: Comments, voluntary shares, profile visits, qualified conversations, contributor benefit, and opportunity.
8. Failure modes: Manufactured controversy, repeated negative openings, claims stronger than evidence, or a vague closing question.
9. Recovery: Challenge the norm only when proof supports a more useful alternative. Otherwise use a different format or remain silent.
10. Strategic implication: The method is a quality lens, not a mandatory costume for every post.

### DECISION F: ONE SHARED SATURATION GOVERNOR

1. Surface: Measure topic, identity, audience, source build, and narrative repetition across Footprint, Rory, Keymon, RedLantern, and products.
2. Root cause: Separate calendars can still overwhelm the same people with coordinated messages.
3. First order effect: Eligible content may be delayed or skipped.
4. Second order effect: The audience sees a useful range of people and topics.
5. Third order effect: Exposure compounds without making the studio appear desperate or artificial.
6. Upstream dependency: Identity mapping, audience circles, content allocation, similarity, and source lineage.
7. Downstream dependency: Scheduling, experimentation, post selection, and learning.
8. Failure modes: The governor rewards inactivity, treats all audiences as identical, or lets one identity consume the budget.
9. Recovery: Use maximums rather than minimum quotas, preserve community allocation, and allow safe silence.
10. Strategic implication: Capture can be continuous while publication remains selective.

## BEHAVIORAL DRIVER SEPARATION

### WHAT CAN EARN A LIKE

ASSUMED: A relevant reader is more likely to react when a post names something they recognize, expresses a useful professional identity, or gives language to an experience they already have.

Design rule: Make the reader feel accurately understood. Do not ask for the reaction.

### WHAT CAN EARN A COMMENT

ASSUMED: A relevant reader is more likely to comment when the post contains useful tension and ends with a question they can answer from direct experience.

Design rule: Ask one narrow question with real answer space. Do not ask readers to agree, tag people, or perform for the algorithm.

### WHAT CAN EARN A SHARE

ASSUMED: A relevant reader is more likely to share when the post helps another person, gives a reusable rule, or lets the sharer add value to their own network.

Design rule: Include a transferable lesson, simple model, checklist, or before and after distinction. Never make sharing a condition of support.

### WHAT CAN EARN A QUALIFIED OPPORTUNITY

ASSUMED: A relevant decision maker is more likely to start a conversation when the public trail shows a repeated ability to understand a specific problem, make good decisions, and produce credible results.

Design rule: Connect each lesson to a clear capability and a real outcome without turning the post into a job request or sales pitch.

## DESTRUCTIVE CRITICISM

### FAILURE PATH 1: AGILE THEATER

Trigger: Agents fill every field after the build merely to satisfy workflow.

Probability: Medium.

Impact: High. The record looks complete while decisions remain untrustworthy.

Mitigation: Capture stage fields before transitions, use evidence references, measure unused fields, and remove fields that change no decision.

Residual risk: Medium because people can still provide low quality inputs.

### FAILURE PATH 2: CONTRARIAN PERFORMANCE

Trigger: Challenge the norm becomes a required opening for every post.
